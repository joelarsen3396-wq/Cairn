Cairn — How to Tread Here
Tribal Land
You're a guest on sovereign land, held and cared for by its people since time immemorial. Rules here aren't bureaucracy — they're an invitation to respect. Check the tribe's website before visiting; some areas welcome visitors, others are sacred and closed. If you're welcomed in, tread gently, buy local if you can, and leave the land better than you found it.
National Forest
This land belongs to everyone, which means it's held in trust for you — and for everyone after you. Forests like this are working landscapes: recreation, watershed, habitat, and yes, logging, all share space here. Campfires are the big one: check current fire restrictions before you strike a match, especially in the West. Stay on trail, pack out everything, and remember that "multiple use" includes the animals who live here full-time.
National Park
You're in one of the most protected places in America — a promise made generations ago to keep this land unimpaired. The rules are stricter here because the stakes are higher: pets stay on pavement, drones stay grounded, and everything natural stays where it is, down to the pinecones. Crowds are part of park life, so your patience is a gift to everyone. Come at dawn if you can. The land is different when it's quiet.
Wilderness Area
This is land America chose to leave alone — no roads, no machines, no permanent anything. Wilderness is the deepest promise we make to a place. That means you carry everything in and everything out, groups stay small, and even bikes and drones are off-limits. It's harder out here on purpose. The reward is a kind of quiet that doesn't exist anywhere else.
BLM Land
This is some of the most open, least-managed public land there is — vast, often empty, and largely yours to roam. That freedom is the point, and it survives only if visitors self-govern. Dispersed camping is usually allowed, but the desert and sagebrush country heal slowly: a tire track or fire scar can last decades. Camp on durable ground, respect existing fire rings, and leave gates as you found them.
State Land
State parks and forests are the locals' land — often quieter than the famous places, and often where the community actually goes. Rules vary a lot from state to state, so the kiosk at the trailhead is your friend. Your entry fee usually funds the place directly, so pay it gladly. These are the parks that teach kids to love the outdoors.
Private Land
Someone holds this land, and passing through is a privilege, not a right. Trails sometimes cross private parcels thanks to the generosity of owners — a handshake agreement that one bad visitor can end. Stay on the easement, leave gates as you found them, and if you meet the owner, a wave and a thank-you go further than you'd think.
